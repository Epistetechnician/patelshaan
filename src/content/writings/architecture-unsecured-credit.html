The Architecture of Unsecured Credit

2025 • Credit & Financial Infrastructure

The central moral failure of overcollateralized DeFi is easy to miss because the system works so cleanly on its own terms. If every borrower must already possess substantial collateral, then lending becomes less about extending productive capacity and more about offering leverage to people who are already capitalized.

That is useful, but it is not credit in the fuller economic sense. It does not meaningfully serve the founder with revenue but no liquid treasury, the worker collective with reliable contracts but no balance sheet, or the real business that needs financing to grow before it can accumulate the assets a DeFi market demands upfront.

The question that keeps pulling me forward is simple: what would it take to make on-chain credit useful for entities whose future cash flows matter more than their present collateral?

Why This Problem Matters

Credit is one of the oldest technologies for translating trust in the future into productive action now. If crypto never solves anything beyond overcollateralized lending, it will remain excellent at serving traders, treasuries, and already-wealthy balance sheets while doing relatively little for the broader economy.

That is why I pay attention to projects that at least gesture toward real-world underwriting. Even when the models are not fully permissionless or fully unsecured, they widen the design space. For example, Goldfinch is useful because it explicitly tries to bring real-world credit exposure onchain, and borrower examples such as Tugende show what it looks like when financing maps to real businesses and income generating assets rather than purely crypto-native collateral loops.

"The world's leading private credit funds - now onchain."
- Goldfinch

I do not cite that because I think institutional private credit is the final form of on-chain finance. I cite it because it signals a broader truth: the moment DeFi starts touching real borrowers, underwriting questions become much more interesting than collateral ratios alone.

The Three Missing Pillars

Traditional unsecured credit usually relies on some combination of identity, history, and enforcement. The borrower is known, their prior behavior is observable, and default carries consequences that extend beyond a single loan. In open crypto systems, all three pillars weaken at once.

1. Identity Becomes Probabilistic

Onchain, identity is not binary. It is a pattern. Which addresses recur? What contracts do they interact with? Which credentials, attestations, counterparties, and revenue streams surround them? For many borrowers, especially digital-native ones, the right question is not "Who are you in the legal-bureau sense?" but "What evidence exists that you are a durable economic actor worth pricing?"

That means the future of unsecured credit likely depends on blended identity systems: some cryptographic, some social, some reputational, some legal.

2. Cash Flow Matters More Than Static Wealth

This is the design principle I return to most often. Good lending is usually about repayment capacity, not symbolic balance-sheet prestige. If a protocol has stable fee revenue, if a business has recurring receivables, or if a cooperative has durable contracts, those streams should matter in underwriting.

In practice, this points toward mechanisms such as revenue escrows, programmable repayment waterfalls, treasury-attached credit lines, and covenant systems that respond to changing performance. Collateral is still useful. It just should not be the only language the market can hear.

3. Memory Has to Become Shared Infrastructure

Credit works because defaults travel. If a borrower burns one lender, the next lender should know. Crypto still lacks a robust, broadly trusted memory layer for default, workout history, restructuring behavior, and context-rich performance data.

Without shared memory, every new lender is underwriting in partial darkness. That pushes markets back toward excessive collateralization because collateral becomes the easiest substitute for institutional memory.

How I Think About Halo

The architecture I am interested in for Halo is not "debt without consequences." It is more like risk made legible and modular. The aim is to compose several imperfect signals into a financing system that can serve more real borrowers than today's DeFi primitives do.

That suggests a few design commitments:

  • reputation-aware underwriting instead of anonymous sameness
  • cash-flow-linked repayment paths instead of pure liquidation logic
  • shared default registries so behavior has durable consequences
  • structured tranching so different risk appetites can coexist inside one market

Why Tranches Still Matter

I do not think risk can be eliminated. I do think it can be organized more intelligently. Tranching remains one of the clearest ways to separate investor preferences:

  • senior capital wants protection and lower volatility
  • junior capital accepts first-loss risk in exchange for higher yield
  • equity-like participants often care about upside, governance, and model belief

TradFi has used this logic for a long time. The opportunity in crypto is not to pretend tranching is new. It is to make tranche behavior more transparent, more programmable, and potentially more composable with the rest of an open financial stack.

The Part That Should Make Us Uncomfortable

Unsecured credit can easily reproduce the worst instincts of traditional finance: opaque scoring, exclusion by proxy, extractive terms, and governance structures that socialize losses while privatizing upside. If we build this category carelessly, we will not have made finance more humane. We will have simply rebuilt old power relations on faster rails.

That is why I think borrower visibility matters so much. We should be able to answer, concretely, who a credit system is for. A small exporter. A protocol treasury with reliable fee flows. A local lender financing productive assets. A worker network with verifiable receivables. If the borrower is always abstract, the system will drift toward financial engineering for its own sake.

The Road Ahead

On-chain unsecured credit will not emerge from one breakthrough primitive. It will likely emerge from layered improvements in identity, underwriting, recoveries, risk segmentation, and reporting. The task is not to eliminate uncertainty. It is to make uncertainty measurable enough that capital can move toward productive use instead of idling behind excess collateral.

If DeFi wants to become infrastructure for a broader economy, this is one of the categories it eventually has to face. Otherwise we remain excellent at collateralizing wealth and mediocre at financing possibility.
