P2P Infrastructure for Humanitarian Aid

2024 • Humanitarian Infrastructure

In humanitarian systems, delay is not an abstract inefficiency. It becomes a parent borrowing at predatory rates for one more week. A student leaving school because rent is due now, not after a compliance chain clears. A family making impossible tradeoffs because aid exists on paper but not yet in hand.

That is why I care about payment rails in crisis contexts. Not because crypto is fashionable, and not because stablecoins are philosophically pure. I care because when formal channels fail or stall, people still need to move value. The infrastructure question becomes painfully literal.

The World Food Programme's cash transfer work captures something important about the human stakes:

"WFP sends people money to give them the flexibility to choose what they need, when they need it."
- World Food Programme

I keep coming back to that phrasing because it is modest and radical at the same time. It assumes that people closest to a crisis generally know more about their own needs than a remote bureaucracy does. That should be obvious. Too often, our systems behave as if it is not.

The Case for P2P Rails

In many regions under financial stress, the formal banking layer is not the most reliable one. Informal and semi-formal peer-to-peer markets often clear faster, with fewer assumptions, because people are already using them to live. Stablecoins become useful here not as an ideological replacement for all money but as an intermediate settlement layer between donors and local liquidity.

That is the intuition behind InstaCash: do not try to build a parallel world from scratch. Bridge into the systems that are already working under pressure. If local P2P conversion networks are more responsive than correspondent banking channels, then humanitarian infrastructure should be designed with that fact in mind.

Why Traditional Aid Rails Often Underperform

Traditional aid pipelines are layered for understandable reasons: compliance, reporting, fraud prevention, partner management, donor constraints. But each layer adds time, interpretation, and handoff risk. Those costs are not merely operational. They reshape who is allowed to receive help fast enough for that help to matter.

The typical chain looks something like this:

  • donor to institution
  • institution to regional operator
  • regional operator to local partner
  • local partner to end recipient

Each handoff introduces waiting, currency friction, and distance from the person's actual situation. Some of that overhead is necessary. Much of it is inherited from systems built to protect institutions from risk more than people from delay.

What a Faster Flow Can Look Like

The architecture I care about is more direct:

  1. Ingest a donation through a familiar donor interface
  2. Convert into a stable settlement asset
  3. Route across a low-fee rail with strong local liquidity
  4. Cash out through trusted P2P networks or recipient support infrastructure
  5. Notify both operators and recipients so the human loop stays alive

For InstaCash, that has meant choices that look pragmatic rather than pure: donor-friendly intake, automated conversion, batching where it actually saves money, and destination chains such as Tron where stablecoin liquidity and fees can make small transfers feasible.

This is not a maximalist vision of crypto. It is a service-oriented one. Choose the rail that minimizes total harm for the person at the receiving end.

What Must Stay Human

I do not believe fully automated humanitarian finance is desirable. The system can verify that a donation arrived, that a conversion executed, and that a transfer hit a wallet. It cannot, on its own, answer the hardest questions:

  • Is the recipient who they claim to be?
  • Is the local off-ramp trustworthy and fair?
  • Is the cash arriving in a context where using it is safe?
  • What secondary support does the recipient need after the transfer?

This is why I think the right framing is not "replace human institutions with code." It is "automate the mechanical bottlenecks so human trust networks can focus on care, verification, and context."

Compliance Is Real, But So Is the Cost of Delay

Anyone building here has to take sanctions screening, AML, money transmission exposure, destination-country rules, and partner due diligence seriously. These constraints are not optional. They shape what is possible.

But I also think the humanitarian sector sometimes under-prices the damage done by slowness. A system can be formally compliant and still be operationally cruel if it takes too long to reach people in need. We should evaluate risk on both sides: the risk of misuse and the risk of institutional delay.

The Larger Use Case

The deeper lesson here is that crypto becomes most morally legible when it is boring in the right way. Not a speculative asset. Not an identity badge. A bridge. A rail. A tool that helps value move across broken boundaries when more familiar systems become unreliable.

I want more humanitarian infrastructure built with that spirit. Faster where it needs to be fast. Local where it needs to be local. Legible enough for donors, safe enough for operators, and flexible enough for recipients to exercise actual choice.

If we are serious about crisis response, then payment design is not back-office work. It is part of the ethics of care.
