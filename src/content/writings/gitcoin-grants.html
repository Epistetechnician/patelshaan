Gitcoin and the Evolution of Public Goods Funding

2023 • DAOs & Capital Distribution

The feeling I associate most with Gitcoin is not ideology. It is a browser with too many tabs open. One tab has a grants page full of fragile, useful, deeply unglamorous work: libraries, security tooling, research, local community projects, documentation. Another tab has the broader market doing what markets do best: rewarding attention, velocity, and spectacle.

Gitcoin matters because it tried to close that gap. It did not merely ask people to "care more" about public goods. It built a mechanism that turns caring into allocative power. That is a much harder and much more interesting design problem.

The official Gitcoin Grants program frames itself as one of web3's longest-running grants systems, and that longevity matters. Many people can describe public goods. Far fewer can build the repetitive, tedious, politically exposed machinery required to fund them quarter after quarter.

Why Quadratic Funding Felt Like a Breakthrough

The core promise of quadratic funding was elegant: broaden the meaning of support. Instead of letting one whale speak louder than everyone else, reward the projects that many people are willing to back, even in small amounts.

Gitcoin described quadratic funding as "the mathematically optimal way to fund an ecosystem in a democratic way."
- Gitcoin Grants

The beauty of that idea is not just mathematical. It is civic. It says that legitimacy can come from distributed conviction rather than concentrated capital. In a space obsessed with venture logic, that felt like a genuine opening.

And for a while, it changed what was visible. Projects that would otherwise have remained invisible became legible to the ecosystem. Maintainers learned to tell the story of why their work mattered. Donors learned that even small contributions could have leverage. The category "digital public goods" stopped being a nice phrase and became a place capital could actually move.

What Gitcoin Got Unambiguously Right

First, it made public goods fundable without requiring a central gatekeeper to bless every project. That is not a small shift. Traditional grant systems are slow, opaque, and often culturally distant from the builders they fund. Gitcoin replaced some of that top-down certainty with participatory uncertainty, and I think that was healthy.

Second, it built reusable infrastructure. Gitcoin did not just run rounds; it helped create a playbook. The mechanics of application review, matching pools, round design, and identity scoring became part of a broader ecosystem vocabulary. Even people who criticize Gitcoin are often thinking in concepts Gitcoin helped popularize.

Third, it made builders feel seen. This is harder to quantify, but it matters. Shared infrastructure work is often emotionally invisible. You only notice it when it fails. A good grants round sends a different signal: someone noticed the maintenance, the bug fixes, the docs, the thankless glue work.

Where the Ideal Meets the Internet

My biggest lesson from participating around these systems is that good funding mechanisms do not operate outside social dynamics. They are submerged inside them. Which means quadratic funding does not escape popularity contests. It restructures them.

Identity Is Always the Open Wound

QF implicitly wants one person to mean one meaningful signal. But pseudonymous environments are adversarial by default. That is why systems such as Gitcoin's surrounding grants infrastructure and related protocol efforts increasingly had to care about sybil resistance, not just funding theory.

Gitcoin Passport is an understandable response: accumulate enough signals that faking personhood becomes expensive. But this is an arms race, not a solved problem. Every identity system creates both protection and exclusion. Every layer of fraud prevention risks filtering out real people who are least fluent in the tooling.

Attention Still Wins More Than We Admit

The toughest rounds taught me that "community support" and "actual impact" do not always travel together. Some of the most important infrastructure in a stack is almost impossible to narrate. Meanwhile, a project with better memes, a louder founder, or a more engaged audience can outperform on the dimension the mechanism can actually see.

This is not a reason to reject QF. It is a reason to be honest about what it measures. QF is good at surfacing broad enthusiasm. It is much weaker at recognizing quiet dependency: the unsexy tool that a thousand other projects rely on but few donors think to celebrate directly.

Rounds Encourage Episodic Behavior

A grants round is a season. That makes it energizing, but it can also distort behavior. Teams learn to optimize for launch moments, campaign windows, and donor activation. The work of maintaining public goods, on the other hand, is not seasonal. It is repetitive, compounding, and often boring.

This is one reason I have become more interested in mechanisms that complement QF instead of replacing it. Streaming support, delegated budgets, and retroactive rewards all address weaknesses that show up when everything is compressed into one highly visible round.

Why Retro Funding Changed the Conversation

What I appreciate about Optimism's Retro Funding work is that it asks a slightly different question. Instead of "What can rally a crowd right now?" it asks "What already created value for the ecosystem?" That matters because public goods are often easier to recognize in hindsight than in advance.

Optimism describes its guiding principle as "impact = profit."
- Retro Funding 2025

I like that phrase because it names the underlying dream of the whole space: if markets fail to reward public goods, then build adjacent mechanisms that do. But even Optimism's own writing is refreshingly honest that measuring impact is messy, politically charged, and still unreliable in important ways.

That honesty is healthy. Public goods funding gets worse when we pretend the mechanism is objective. It gets better when we admit that judgment, context, and institutional memory still matter.

What I Think Comes Next

I no longer believe in a single best mechanism. I believe in a stack:

  • quadratic funding for broad early signal
  • retroactive funding for demonstrated ecosystem value
  • delegated or committee-based grants for specialized evaluation
  • streaming support for long-lived maintenance work
  • identity systems that reduce abuse without hardening into exclusion

I also think we need to keep funding culture, not just code. A healthy public goods ecosystem is made of translators, conveners, reviewers, maintainers, educators, and community operators in addition to protocol engineers. Gitcoin helped widen that aperture. The next generation of funding systems should push it wider.

The deepest value Gitcoin created may not be any specific round. It may be the shared intuition that infrastructure can be collectively funded at all. Once a community has seen that happen, even imperfectly, it becomes much harder to go back to the old assumption that public goods must survive on vibes alone.
