Sacred Economics in Cryptographic Systems

2024 • Sacred Economics & Alternative Systems

The contradiction usually hits me during public-goods season. I am donating to open-source tools, reading project updates from teams that keep shared infrastructure alive, and feeling genuinely moved by the idea that a digital commons can fund itself. Then I open the next tab and see the rest of crypto doing what it often does best: extraction dressed up as revolution.

That split matters to me because I still believe crypto can become a moral technology instead of merely a financial one. But that will not happen just because we use wallets, signatures, and smart contracts. It will only happen if we encode different social relations into the systems themselves.

Charles Eisenstein's Sacred Economics remains one of the cleanest lenses I know for naming what is wrong with our default money story. The book's premise is not simply that markets are bad or that gift is good. It is that money shapes social reality, and our current money system has trained us into a specific kind of distance from one another.

"Sacred Economics traces the history of money from ancient gift economies to modern capitalism, revealing how the money system has contributed to alienation, competition, and scarcity, destroyed community, and necessitated endless growth."
- Sacred Economics

The reason I keep returning to that framing is that crypto changes the medium of coordination without necessarily changing the logic. We replaced banks with protocols, but too often kept the same emotional architecture: scarcity, accumulation, status games, and exit over stewardship. If protocol design is a kind of social architecture, then most of our systems still feel like luxury condominiums built on top of a commons we barely know how to protect.

Money Is Not Neutral Code

A lot of crypto discourse treats money like software plumbing. Faster rails, lower fees, better settlement, more expressive assets. All of that matters, but it misses the more consequential question: what habits of relating are we rewarding?

A system that rewards hoarding tells one story about human nature. A system that rewards circulation tells another. A system that makes it easy to extract from a network but hard to contribute back tells its own story too. Tokens are never just tokens. They are invitations into a moral imagination.

That is why I think protocol design is also narrative design. The rules we encode become the emotional weather of a community: what people optimize for, what feels admirable, what feels foolish, and what kinds of obligations become visible.

Where I Actually See the Possibility

I do not think sacred economics will arrive as one grand protocol that fixes everything. I see it more as a direction of travel, visible in partial experiments that get some piece of the puzzle right.

Circles is interesting to me because it does not begin from the fantasy that money should be created only by states or banks. It begins from the possibility that communities can participate directly in monetary creation and trust formation.

"A money commons is a counter-movement against financialization."
- Circles UBI Handbook

I do not think Circles is a finished answer. But I appreciate that it tries to make money relational rather than merely exchangeable. It points toward a world in which trust, local reciprocity, and social memory matter as much as raw balance-sheet power.

I also see real sacred-economic instincts in Gitcoin Grants and Optimism's retro funding experiments. Neither is perfect. Both are vulnerable to gaming, narrative capture, and governance fatigue. But they do something important: they make room for the idea that infrastructure, maintenance, and shared benefit deserve direct reward. That is already a different civilizational posture than pure speculation.

Gift, But Not Naivete

One mistake people make when they hear "gift" is assuming it means softness, vagueness, or the disappearance of accountability. I mean something more demanding. Gift economies are not frictionless. They are thick with memory, reputation, and obligation. They ask us to keep track of who is showing up for the commons and who is merely passing through it.

On-chain systems can help with that. They can preserve social memory, coordinate public accounting, and make reciprocal contribution legible across time. But we should resist flattening everything into metrics. The deepest forms of value in a community are often the least legible: maintenance, translation, care work, conflict mediation, and the patient labor of helping a system stay humane as it scales.

Demurrage, Circulation, and the Limits of Number-Go-Up

Eisenstein's interest in negative interest and demurrage still feels radical because it attacks one of the most basic instincts in modern finance: the moral prestige of holding. In crypto, where people are trained to measure intelligence by how early they were and how long they can sit on an asset, demurrage feels almost heretical.

And yet I keep coming back to the intuition behind it. If money is supposed to support life, then perpetual accumulation cannot be its highest purpose. Circulation matters. Reuse matters. Reinvestment into the commons matters. Even if few large-scale token systems adopt demurrage directly, we can still build adjacent mechanisms that express the same ethic:

  • protocol fees that automatically replenish public-goods pools
  • treasury policies that privilege productive deployment over passive hoarding
  • local or mission-specific currencies optimized for use rather than storage
  • retroactive rewards for maintenance work that markets underprice

The Cypherpunk Tension Is Real

I do not want sacred economics to become a moral excuse for surveillance or forced intimacy. One of crypto's deepest gifts is that it protects exit. It gives people tools to route around coercive institutions. Privacy and autonomy matter, especially for people living under fragile or predatory systems.

So the challenge is not to build compulsory community. It is to build systems that make generosity, reciprocity, and stewardship easier without making them mandatory or totalizing. The best version of sacred economics in crypto is not anti-cypherpunk. It is cypherpunk infrastructure in service of more voluntary, more caring, and more durable forms of coordination.

What I Want More Of

I want fewer protocols that confuse extraction with innovation. I want more systems that reward the people who maintain shared infrastructure long after the hype has left. I want token economies that make contribution feel honorable and depletion feel embarrassing.

Most of all, I want us to stop pretending that software design is morally neutral. Every treasury policy, emissions schedule, governance mechanic, and incentive layer encodes a theory of the human person. Sacred economics asks us to choose that theory more carefully.

"The new story of money is a story of reunion, reunion with the community of all life on earth."
- Charles Eisenstein

I do not think crypto becomes worthy by being more efficient than the old system. I think it becomes worthy when it helps us remember that economies are not machines for accumulation. They are living arrangements about how we hold one another.
